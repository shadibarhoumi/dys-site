about designing your stanford

What are the basics?

Designing Your Stanford is a sophomore-only 2-unit class, graded “credit / no credit”. During Fall Quarter 2013, class will take place on Thursdays from 3:15-5:05pm in the Meyer Forum Room. We can accommodate approximately 70 students.

How much work will it be?

Beyond the 2 hours of class every week, students can expect 1-3 hours of homework most weeks, with a slightly heavier load during weeks 6-9. There will generally be some light reading and videos, but the majority of the homework involves going out into campus and doing things (e.g. connecting with professors or other students, visiting a research center or group meeting, exploring campus resources) and then reflecting on those experiences. Reflections will include written and multimedia formats with the final goal of creating a portfolio or curated collection of experiences and reflections.

I see “design” in the course name. Do we learn Design Thinking?

Yes! We will teach you the core concepts of Design Thinking and work through a number of exercises and applications of those ideas to your own life and college experience.

Who teaches Designing Your Stanford?

DYS is taught by a team of four amazing instructors: Bill Burnett is Executive Director of the Design Program at Stanford and has years of Silicon Valley experience including managing the PowerBook line at Apple and running his own design Consultancy. Dave Evans has been on a mission to help others find and pursue theirs ever since his undergrad days at Stanford. Along the way, he co-founded Electronic Arts and introduced the computer mouse to the world at Apple. Eugene Korsunskiy and Kyle Williams graduated from the Stanford Design program in 2012 and joined forces with Bill and Dave to co-create and teach Designing Your Stanford.

What is Designing Your Stanford really about?

DYS is about employing a certain kind of “how to do college” (not a “what” to do or “why” to do it). We use a process and a set of behaviors rooted in Design Thinking that, when implemented, can lead to a more authentic and satisfying college experience. Itʼs a creative authentication approach focused on personal engagement not a maximization approach focused on doing more things or an optimization approach focused on finding the right or best way to do Stanford—that is, itʼs not a destination or a goal. The DYS student recognizes that she may not yet have fully resolved the purpose and specific goals of her college education and thus seeks to balance directed focus and serendipitous exploration.

So, what will we be doing, exactly?

In DYS, you will go through a process of deliberately, conscientiously, and experimentally engaging your path through college, rather than simply “doing college”. In most cases, your path will be self-defined at least in part, though one could engage an entirely traditional path with a DYS mindset.

Sounds great! How does it work?

We use a problem-based learning approach that involves 3 core practices.

Engagement: Implementing the “bias to action” mindset of Design Thinking, we go out into the world and prototype by doing things, instead of just trying to think harder to figure out what a given choice might bring. We strive to always have “skin in the game” and avoid “just going through the motions.

Reflection: We synthesize experiences, ideas, and thoughts from the prototypes to guide a course of action. The DYS student learns to discern his own voice and builds awareness for his intuitive as well as analytical response and takeaways in a situation.

Storytelling: We gather artifacts, collecting them into a portfolio from which we can weave a body of compelling narratives about our college experience. This serves both to gain clarity for the student herself during her college navigation, as well as to communicate her story to peers, educators, and potential employers upon completion of college.

What do I get / whatʼs the benefit/outcome? How would senior year be different?

An increased likelihood of ensuring that youʼre getting as much out of Stanford as youʼre putting into it. By the time you near graduation, youʼll have full access to all your college outcomes and an enhanced ability to translate them into useful stories that facilitate opportunities. Perhaps most importantly youʼll have learned how to “wayfind” through uncharted territory balancing both getting things done and exploring new discoveries.
